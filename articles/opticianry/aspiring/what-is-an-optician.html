What Is an Optician, and What Do They Do?

Category: Aspiring Student (Opticianry) | Published: November 26, 2025

A Clear Guide for Future Opticianry Students in Canada

If you love helping people see better, enjoy working with your hands, and want a healthcare career that lets you talk to patients every day without going to university for eight years, opticianry might be perfect for you.

So, who is an optician in Canada?

An **optician** is the eyecare professional who takes the glasses or contact lens prescription written by an optometrist or ophthalmologist and turns it into something the patient can actually wear and see perfectly with.

In most provinces, opticians are licensed and regulated (British Columbia, Alberta, Saskatchewan, Manitoba, Ontario, Quebec, New Brunswick, Nova Scotia, PEI, and Newfoundland & Labrador). That license means you’re recognized as a true healthcare professional, not just a retail worker.

What does a Canadian optician actually do every day?

Help Patients Choose the Perfect Frames and Lenses

You listen to how they use their eyes (driving at night, lots of computer work, sports, fashion) and recommend frames that look great and feel comfortable. You also suggest lens options: thin high-index lenses, anti-reflective coatings, transitions that darken in the sun, blue-light protection, progressives, etc.

Take Precise Measurements

You measure exactly where the centre of the pupil is in the frame, how far the lenses should sit from the eyes, the curve of the frame on the face, and the tilt of the lenses. Even **one millimetre off** can cause headaches or blurry vision, so this part is a skilled craft.

Fit and Adjust Glasses

When the glasses come back from the lab, you make sure they sit perfectly on the nose and ears. A quick twist of the pliers and suddenly the glasses stop sliding down or pinching behind the ears. Patients love this part.

Fit Contact Lenses

In every regulated province, licensed opticians can fit and dispense contact lenses. You teach first-time wearers how to put them in and take them out, check that they fit the curve of the eye properly, and solve problems like dry eyes or lenses that move too much.

Repair and Maintain Glasses

Fix bent frames, replace nose pads, tighten tiny screws, or re-cut a lens that chipped. Many patients say the optician who can fix their favourite broken glasses in five minutes is their hero.

Troubleshoot Vision Problems Caused by the Glasses

If someone says “these new glasses are giving me headaches,” you figure out if the prescription was edged wrong, the lenses are too high or low, or the frame is warped. You fix it or get it remade.

Run or Work in the Optical Store

Many opticians end up managing the entire eyewear side of a clinic: ordering stock, dealing with labs, training staff, and making sure every patient leaves happy.

Where do Canadian opticians work?

The work environments are highly diverse:

  • Independent optical stores
  • Big chains (like Hakim, Specsavers, New Look, or FYI Doctors)
  • Inside Costco, Walmart, or LensCrafters
  • Clinics owned by optometrists
  • Some even open their own boutiques or travel to nursing homes to help seniors who can’t leave

How long is the training?

  • **2–3 years of college** (programs at Georgian College in Ontario, Vancouver Community College in BC, Collège Édouard-Montpetit in Quebec, and others)
  • Includes paid **co-op or practicum placements** where you work in real optical stores
  • After graduation, you write national and provincial licensing exams
  • Once licensed, you can work anywhere in Canada (except territories that aren’t regulated yet)

What’s the vibe like?

You’re on your feet, talking to people all day, solving little puzzles (“why is this lens making a rainbow?”), and getting instant gratitude when someone puts on their new glasses and says **“WOW, everything is so sharp!”**

It’s part science, part fashion, part customer service, and 100% about making someone’s daily life better.

Why new students love it

  • You start earning a good salary right after a two- or three-year program
  • Tons of jobs — almost every town in Canada needs licensed opticians
  • Flexible hours and locations
  • You still get to work in healthcare and help people see their grandkids’ faces clearly, read, drive safely, or play sports
  • Many opticians later open their own stores and become their own boss

If you’re the kind of person who loves making people smile the moment they put on their new glasses, opticianry could be your future career.

Your next move? Book a tour at your nearest college opticianry program or ask to shadow at a local optical store for a day. One afternoon behind the dispensing counter and you’ll know if this is your thing.

What Is an Optometrist, and What Do They Do?

Category: Aspiring Student (Optometry) | Published: November 25, 2025

A Straightforward Guide for Future Optometry Students in Canada

If you’re thinking about applying to the **University of Waterloo** or **Université de Montréal**, you’ve probably heard that optometrists “check eyes and prescribe glasses.” That’s true — but it’s only about **20% of the job**. In Canada, optometrists are the main eye doctors for most people, and the role is far more medical and independent than most pre-optometry students expect.

So who exactly is an optometrist in Canada?

An optometrist is a university-trained eye doctor who graduates with a **Doctor of Optometry (O.D.)** degree. Once licensed by your provincial college (the regulatory body in each province), you become the go-to professional for almost everything related to eyes and vision — unless someone needs actual eye surgery.

What does a Canadian optometrist actually do every day?

Full Eye Health Check-Ups

You do way more than ask “which is better, one or two?” You examine the front and back of the eye, take detailed photos of the retina, check eye pressure, and test how well the eyes work together. You see babies as young as six months, kids, adults, and seniors.

Prescribe Glasses and Contact Lenses

Everyday glasses, progressives (no-line bifocals), contacts for astigmatism, or specialty lenses for unusually shaped eyes — you decide what each patient needs.

Find and Treat Eye Diseases Yourself

Canadian optometrists can diagnose and manage almost all common eye diseases without sending the patient to a specialist first. That includes:

  • **Glaucoma** (increased pressure that can damage the optic nerve)
  • Diabetic eye changes
  • **Age-related macular degeneration** (the leading cause of blindness in older adults)
  • Infections, inflammation, and dry eye problems
  • Urgent issues like sudden floaters or flashes that could mean a retinal tear

Prescribe Medications

In every province, optometrists are allowed to prescribe eye drops and oral medications. That means you can give antibiotic drops for an eye infection, steroid drops for inflammation, pills for shingles in the eye, or daily drops to lower eye pressure in glaucoma patients — all on the spot. (The official term is **“therapeutic pharmaceutical agents,”** which just means “prescription drugs for treating eye conditions.”)

Do Small In-Office Procedures

Remove a piece of metal stuck in someone’s eye, insert tiny plugs to help severe dry eye, treat ingrown eyelashes, or drain a painful eyelid cyst — all with just numbing drops, no operating room needed.

Help Kids and People with Special Vision Needs

Many optometrists specialize in children’s vision, slowing down childhood nearsightedness, or running **vision therapy** programs for kids (or adults) whose eyes don’t team up properly.

Work in Hospitals or Remote Communities

Some optometrists work alongside specialists in hospitals or fly into **First Nations communities** where they’re the only eye doctor for hundreds of kilometres.

Where will you work after graduation?

The variety of practice settings is wide open:

  • Your own **private practice**
  • A **group clinic** (very common in Canada)
  • Inside a **retail optical store** but still fully independent clinically
  • Hospitals, rehab centres, or the **Canadian Armed Forces**
  • Research and teaching at **Waterloo** or in private industry

How independent are you?

Extremely. If a patient walks in with high eye pressure and early glaucoma, you start the treatment drops that same visit in every province. No mandatory referral first. Patients often come to their optometrist before their family doctor for red eyes, sudden vision loss, or floaters — and you handle it.

Why this should excite you

When you graduate in Canada, you’re not **“just the glasses person.”** You’re a primary eye-care doctor who prevents blindness, treats disease, runs a business (if you want), and still gets to see a kid light up when they see the world clearly for the first time.

If the idea of combining science, patient care, technology, and real medical responsibility sounds like your dream career, optometry in Canada is worth a serious look.

Your next step? Shadow a few optometrists near you. One day in a busy clinic will show you more than any article ever could.
